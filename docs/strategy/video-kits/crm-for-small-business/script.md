**[WHAT YOU'RE ACTUALLY BUYING]**

Before the list — what you're really trying to buy here is a place to keep every lead and
customer so nobody gets forgotten. Keep that in mind, because a CRM only helps if
something is reliably putting leads into it in the first place. A CRM stores the people
who already reached you. Whether you're actually catching everyone who tries to reach you
is a different question — we'll come back to that at the end.

**[CHAPTER 1 — GoHighLevel]**

Number one, and the biggest toolbox on this list: GoHighLevel, from about 97 dollars a
month, with an AI Employee add-on running another 50 to 97 dollars a month. It's built for
agencies — CRM plus a full funnel and email-campaign builder, with a huge library of
ready-made templates you can resell under your own brand.

[B-ROLL: GoHighLevel pricing page]

Here's the honest catch: the AI is a paid extra, not part of the base plan, and the
per-client costs add up fast. People say it takes 2 to 4 weeks to really learn the thing.

**[CHAPTER 2 — HubSpot]**

Number two: HubSpot. It's got a free CRM tier, Starter runs about 15 dollars a seat per
month, and Professional jumps to around 800 dollars a month plus onboarding. This is the
polished, well-known CRM built for bigger companies — some of the best reporting and CRM
detail around, and it connects to a huge number of other tools.

Watch out for this: jumping from Starter to Professional costs about 40 times more, plus
a required setup fee of around 3,000 dollars at that level.

**[CHAPTER 3 — Zoho CRM]**

Number three: Zoho CRM, from about 14 to 20 dollars a user per month. It's a deep,
customizable CRM that costs far less than the big-company options, and it's part of a
45-app suite with a built-in Zia AI helper on higher plans.

The catch: to get the real value, you have to combine several Zoho apps together. It's a
toolkit, not a ready-to-go front office.

**[CHAPTER 4 — Keap]**

Number four: Keap, now owned by Thryv, from about 249 to 299 dollars a month. It's a
long-running small-business CRM built around sales pipelines and invoices, with
well-built marketing automation and a long history of onboarding new customers.

The honest tradeoff: it costs about 3 times more than GoHighLevel's starting price, and
its features are slowly being folded into Thryv over time.

**[CHAPTER 5 — Pipedrive]**

Number five: Pipedrive, from about 14 to 24 dollars a user per month. This is a CRM built
around a visual sales pipeline — a clean screen built around deal stages that a sales team
can learn quickly, with a solid mobile app.

Watch out for this: it's a sales pipeline tool first. No website, no booking calendar, no
receptionist behind it — and the AI features cost extra.

**[CHAPTER 6 — Jobber]**

Number six: Jobber, from about 39 to 69 dollars a month. This is software for running
home-service jobs, with a CRM built specifically for how trades work — quotes,
scheduling, and invoices all in one place, plus a hub where customers can help
themselves.

The catch: it's a job-management tool, not a lead-capture front office. It assumes the
lead already called you — there's no AI receptionist or website builder here.

And number one on this list — full disclosure again, this is the one I build:
SeldonFrame. A CRM is just a customer list. SeldonFrame is a whole front office: an AI
receptionist that answers calls, texts, and chats, a website, a booking calendar and
forms — all writing into the same customer list on their own.

Where the others win: if you're an agency running funnels and email campaigns for

**[WHAT ABOUT FREE CRMS]**

Quick honest note on free CRMs — they exist. HubSpot's free plan is the best-known one,
and it's genuinely usable to start. But "free" almost always means a small cap on
contacts, no automatic follow-up, and a push toward paid plans the moment you need more
than storing a name and a phone number. The real goal is never missing a lead, not just
having a place to write one down — a free CRM by itself still won't answer the phone or
send the follow-up text.
