How to sell AI agents: nobody wrote the guides, so my agents shipped 32 of them in 2 days

Everyone writes for the people buying agents. This is the build for the people selling them. Including the moment my reviewer agent accused my writer agent of fabricating a statistic it had actually verified.

There's a gap hiding in plain sight. Search for advice on buying AI automation and you'll drown in it. Now search for how to sell an AI agent. How to price it. Where to list it. What marketplace fees actually run. How to white-label one build across ten clients. It goes quiet, fast.

I noticed because I audited my own SEO estate this week. 300+ pages talking to buyers. Basically zero talking to the builders who create supply. For a marketplace, that's a store with a beautiful front door and no vendor entrance.

So I spent two days fixing it. 32 guides, live, every claim traced to a primary source. Here's how the factory ran. Including the part where quality control turned on itself.

The plan came first, and it was the slow part

The tempting move was obvious. Generate 40 articles, publish, done. That's also how you get the gray sludge choking search results right now. And sludge would be fatal for me specifically, because my whole positioning is that my product never lies to you. AI filler under a never-lies banner isn't just bad content. It's a contradiction that kills the brand.

So the human hours went into a plan of record. 40 articles across 8 waves, each wave one coherent cluster: pricing, marketplaces and fees, white-labeling, client acquisition for agent builders. Every article got an intent statement, a keyword, and one hard rule: numbers come from primary sources or they get cut.

One wave got frozen before it started. Wave 8 is case studies, and I have opinions but only a handful of closed deals. So the rule I wrote into the plan: case studies wait until ten real closes exist. No composite customers. No imaginary plumber named Dave. The gap on my sitemap is the receipt that the other 32 aren't invented.

Then the waves ran

Wave 1: six guides, one pull request, reviewed and merged. Then waves 2 through 7, back to back. Write, review, revise, merge, next.

Each wave got drafted by a writer agent, then checked by a separate reviewer agent. Never the same one. The maker never grades its own homework. Then I made the merge call.

The rhythm is the point here. This isn't "AI wrote my blog." It's a production line with stations. Plan, that's me. Draft, agent. Adversarial review, different agent. Merge call, me again. My hands-on time per wave was minutes, and I spent it on judgment: does this cluster hold together, is this claim sourced, is the tone drifting.

By the end: 32 guides live, search engines pinged, and a queue file that tells next week exactly where the line stopped.

The reviewer that cried wolf

Best moment of the build. I'm still thinking about it.

Mid-wave, the reviewer agent flagged a guide with a BLOCKING verdict. The claim: 97% of consumers read reviews, 85% influenced by positive ones, attributed to a 2026 survey. The reviewer called it a classic hallucination fingerprint. And honestly? Textbook catch. Suspiciously round numbers, convenient year, exactly the shape of stat these models love to invent.

Except I opened the primary source myself. Every number was on the page. Literally, including the year. The writer agent had fetched the source live before citing it. The reviewer, pattern-matching on what fake stats usually look like, was the one guessing.

Sit with that for a second. The fabrication detector fabricated a fabrication.

I kept the reviewer. In the same batch it caught two real problems, and an over-suspicious checker beats a credulous one every single time. But the incident wrote a new rule into my build system: verify the verifier before acting on its verdict. When a reviewer claims a source problem, I check the source, not the reviewer's confidence. Trusting the writer on faith or trusting the reviewer on faith, it's the same bug wearing different clothes.

What two days cost and bought

Bought: a supply-side moat that compounds. Guides for sellers attract sellers. Sellers stock the marketplace. A stocked marketplace gives buyers a reason to show up. Content that recruits vendors isn't blogging, it's distribution infrastructure.

Cost: mostly attention, honestly. The agent time is cheap. The scarce thing was judgment at the gates. Plan approval, merge calls, the reviewer incident. That's the founder time worth protecting, and the factory design, agents do volume, I do verdicts, is what keeps it to hours instead of weeks.

What I don't know yet, honestly

Zero ranking data exists. The guides went live this week. Indexing takes weeks, ranking takes months. "32 guides live" is a shipping receipt, not a traffic receipt. The search console graph will tell the real story and I'll post it either way.

There's also a gap I can't fill with agents. Wave 8 stays empty until ten real closes happen, and closes come from selling, not writing. The content factory is idle capacity parked against my hardest bottleneck, which is me actually doing sales. The machine ships guides. It can't ship proof.

And my CI was red this week, and it wasn't the waves' fault. A pre-existing flaky test in an unrelated corner failed the same day. I'm flagging it because saying "everything's green" would be a small lie, and small lies are how the big ones get in.

What's next

The queue file says Wave 8 is gated. So next is the unglamorous part: close ten deals, then write the case studies that are currently forbidden. The factory did its job. Now I have to do mine.

The guides are free at seldonframe.com/guides, the "Building & selling AI agents" cluster. If you build agents for clients, that's your section of the store.
